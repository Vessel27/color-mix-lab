Applications of Additive Color (RGB)

Additive mixing occurs when emitted light from different sources is combined. The more light you add, the brighter the result becomes.

  • Digital Screens: Your phone, laptop, and TV use tiny Red, Green, and Blue pixels to create every color you see.
  • Stage Lighting: Concerts and theaters use RGB spotlights to wash the stage in different hues or create pure white light.
  • Projectors: Digital projectors mix light beams to display images on a screen.
  • Vision: Human eyes have receptors (cones) specifically tuned to Red, Green, and Blue wavelengths.

Applications of Subtractive Color (CMY)

Subtractive mixing occurs when pigments or filters absorb (subtract) specific wavelengths of light and reflect others back to your eyes.

  • Color Printing: Printers use Cyan, Magenta, and Yellow (CMYK) inks. Each ink "subtracts" its opposite color from white paper.
  • Painting: Artists mix physical pigments. Adding more colors absorbs more light, eventually resulting in dark muddy tones or black.
  • Photography: Traditional film uses layers of dyes to filter light and produce a colored image.
  • Textiles: Dyeing clothes involves applying pigments that subtract light to give the fabric its specific color.
